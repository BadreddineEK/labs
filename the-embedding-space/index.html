The Embedding Space

Words are not numbers. But to reason about language, a model must make them so. This is the story of how meaning becomes geometry — and why it works.

01

The problem: words aren't numbers

Neural networks compute with numbers. But language is made of words. The naive approach — assign each word an integer — creates a flat encoding where "cat" = 4281 and "dog" = 4282, implying they're barely related. That's wrong.

What we need is a representation where similar words are close to each other, and the relationships between words are preserved geometrically. That's exactly what an embedding is: a dense vector of real numbers encoding meaning in its structure.

🔢
One-hot encoding (naive)
Each word is a vector of zeros with a single 1. With 50k words, each vector has 50k dimensions. No similarity is captured — every word is equidistant from every other.
🧲
Dense embedding (learned)
Each word maps to a compact vector of ~768 floats, learned from context. Similar words cluster together. Opposite concepts sit far apart. Relationships become directions.
📐
Geometry as semantics
In embedding space, arithmetic works on meaning. king − man + woman ≈ queen. The concept of "royalty" is an encodable direction in vector space.

Exploring the space

Embedding spaces are high-dimensional — typically 768 or 1536 dimensions. We project down to 2D using t-SNE or UMAP to visualize structure. The clusters and distances that appear reflect real semantic relationships the model learned from raw text.

📍 Query: king

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors — not their distance. Two words can be far apart in absolute terms but point in the same direction, making them semantically close.

cos(θ) = A · B / (‖A‖ × ‖B‖)
03

Arithmetic on meaning

The most striking property of embedding spaces is that relationships become directions. The vector offset from "man" to "woman" encodes gender. From "France" to "Paris" encodes capital. You can add and subtract these directions and land on semantically correct answers.

Click the colored words to swap them and explore different analogies.

This is a consistent geometric property — not cherry-picked magic. The model learns that "capital of" is a direction in space, just as "plural of" or "past tense of" are directions. Language structure is baked into geometry during training.

04

How we got here

Embeddings didn't arrive fully formed. They evolved over two decades as models learned to capture increasingly rich representations of language.

📊
2003
Neural language models — Bengio et al.
First demonstration that a neural network could learn distributed word representations as a side effect of predicting the next token. The embedding idea was born.
W
2013
Word2Vec — Mikolov et al., Google
Efficient shallow networks (CBOW & Skip-gram) trained on billions of words. First public demonstration of the king − man + woman = queen analogy. Made embeddings accessible to the world.
Breakthrough
G
2014
GloVe — Stanford NLP
Combined global co-occurrence statistics with local context learning, encoding both word frequency and semantic relationships more faithfully than Word2Vec alone.
🔤
2018
ELMo — contextual embeddings
The same word now gets a different embedding depending on its context. "Bank" in "river bank" vs. "national bank" produces two distinct vectors. A fundamental leap.
B
2018–2019
BERT & transformer embeddings
Attention mechanisms let every token attend to every other token. Embeddings became deeply contextual, encoding syntax, semantics, and pragmatics simultaneously.
Breakthrough
🌍
2022 → now
Modern text embeddings — OpenAI, Cohere, Mistral…
Dedicated encoder models with contrastive training objectives. 1536-dimensional vectors that power RAG, semantic search, and classification at scale.
05

What to remember

Five sentences on embeddings.

  1. An embedding is a dense vector of real numbers encoding the meaning of a word, sentence, or document.
  2. Similar meanings are close in vector space — closeness is measured by cosine similarity, not Euclidean distance.
  3. Relationships are directions — you can add and subtract vectors to navigate concepts like gender, tense, or geography.
  4. Modern embeddings are contextual — the same word gets a different vector depending on its surrounding tokens.
  5. Embeddings power everything in modern AI: RAG, semantic search, classification, recommendation, and clustering.

01

Why RAG exists

Large language models are trained on a fixed snapshot of the world. They know a lot — but they don't know your documents, your recent data, or anything that happened after their training cutoff.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation solves this by connecting a model to an external knowledge base at inference time. Instead of asking the model to hallucinate an answer, you first retrieve the relevant context, then ask the model to generate an answer grounded in that context.

🧊
Frozen knowledge
LLMs can't update their weights after training. Without RAG, their knowledge is static.
🔍
Grounded answers
RAG anchors generation in retrieved documents, reducing hallucination significantly.
📦
Your own data
Internal reports, PDFs, code, emails — RAG makes any private corpus queryable.

Step 1 — Chunking

Your source documents are too long to fit in a context window. Chunking splits them into smaller, overlapping pieces so each one can be embedded and searched independently. Chunk size matters: too large = noisy retrieval; too small = missing context.

03

Where RAG fails

RAG is not a silver bullet. Each stage in the pipeline can degrade the final answer quality in a different way.

✂️
Bad chunking
Splitting a sentence mid-way or making chunks too short destroys semantic meaning before embedding even starts.
📐
Wrong embedding model
A general embedding model may not understand your domain vocabulary — use domain-specific models when possible.
🎯
Top-k is arbitrary
Retrieving too many chunks adds noise. Retrieving too few misses context. There's no universal k — it depends on your data.
🗣️
Context window pressure
The LLM's context window is finite. Stuffing too many chunks degrades generation quality. Reranking helps — it's not optional.
🌀
Still hallucinates
RAG reduces hallucination but doesn't eliminate it. The model can still misread or misquote the retrieved context.
🔄
Stale index
Your vector index is a snapshot. If documents are updated and the index isn't re-synced, retrieval returns outdated content.
04

What to remember

RAG in five sentences.

  1. Chunk your documents into semantically coherent pieces — not just fixed-size windows.
  2. Embed every chunk into a high-dimensional vector space using an encoder model.
  3. At query time, embed the user question and find the nearest chunks in vector space.
  4. Rerank the retrieved candidates with a cross-encoder to surface the most relevant ones.
  5. Inject the top chunks into the LLM prompt as context, then let the model generate a grounded answer.

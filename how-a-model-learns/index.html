How a Model Learns

A model doesn't start smart. It starts random, makes mistakes, measures how wrong it was, and adjusts — millions of times. Here's what that actually looks like.

5Concepts

01

What is a loss?

Before a model can improve, it needs to know how wrong it is. The loss function is that measure — a single number that summarizes the gap between what the model predicted and what the correct answer was. The smaller the loss, the better the model.

Common loss functions include Mean Squared Error (MSE) for regression tasks — the average squared difference between predictions and targets — and Cross-Entropy for classification — how surprised the model was by the correct label given its probability distribution.

🎯
The goal
Minimize the loss over the entire training dataset. Every training step is one small move toward a lower loss.
📐
MSE Loss
L = (1/n) Σ(ŷ − y)². Penalizes large errors heavily due to squaring. Standard for regression.
🔀
Cross-Entropy
L = −Σ y·log(ŷ). Measures how confident the model was about the right class. Standard for classification.
02

The loss landscape

Imagine plotting the loss for every possible combination of two weights. You'd get a surface — a loss landscape. The training goal is to find the lowest valley. With millions of parameters, this surface is impossibly high-dimensional, but the intuition stays the same: navigate downhill.

03

Gradient descent

The gradient is the direction of steepest ascent in the loss landscape. To minimize the loss, we move in the opposite direction — downhill. We scale that step by a small constant called the learning rate (η), which controls how far we move each step.

The update rule is simple: w ← w − η · ∇L(w). Too large a learning rate and you overshoot the minimum, bouncing around or diverging. Too small and training takes forever. Finding the right value — or using a scheduler to adapt it — is one of the most important practical decisions in training.

04

Backpropagation

Gradient descent tells us which direction to move. But how do we compute the gradient across millions of parameters in a deep network? Backpropagation is the answer — it applies the chain rule of calculus to efficiently propagate the error signal backwards through every layer.

Backprop is why deep learning is computationally feasible. Without it, computing the gradient for each parameter individually would require n+1 forward passes for a model with n parameters. Backprop does it in just one forward + one backward pass.

05

Learning rate matters

The learning rate is the single most impactful hyperparameter in training. It determines the size of each gradient step. Too high and the optimization diverges; too low and it converges agonizingly slowly or gets stuck in shallow local minima.

Too high
η = 0.9
Loss oscillates wildly. The optimizer overshoots the minimum on every step, unable to converge.
Just right
η = 0.03
Loss decreases smoothly and converges to a low value. This is the sweet spot.
Too low
η = 0.001
Loss decreases, but painfully slowly. Training could take 100x longer to reach the same loss.

Where this fits

Training is the engine that gives meaning to every other concept in this series. Embeddings only become useful after they've been trained. Attention weights only specialize after backprop has tuned them across millions of examples. RAG retrieval only works because the retriever and generator were trained end-to-end.

What to remember

Training in five sentences.

  1. The loss function measures how wrong the model is — a single number the optimizer tries to minimize.
  2. The loss landscape is a high-dimensional surface; training is the process of navigating to its lowest point.
  3. Gradient descent updates weights in the direction opposite to the gradient — always moving downhill.
  4. Backpropagation computes gradients efficiently through the chain rule in one forward + one backward pass.
  5. The learning rate controls step size — the single most impactful hyperparameter in practice.

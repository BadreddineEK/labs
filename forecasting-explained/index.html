What is a forecast?

A forecast is a structured bet about the future, informed by patterns in the past. It is not a crystal ball. It is not a guess. It is a calibrated estimate with known limitations.

"The past is known. The future is estimated."

"A forecast is not a prediction. It is a structured hypothesis."

"The goal is not to be right. It is to be less wrong."

The further you look,

Forecasting is hard for reasons that are structural, not just technical. Understanding these limits is the first step toward better predictions.

The horizon problem

The shock problem

Models are trained on the past. Structural breaks come from the future. When the underlying system changes abruptly, historical patterns become misleading.

"Models are trained on the past. Shocks come from the future."

The noise problem

At low noise, the underlying trend is obvious. As randomness increases, signal and noise become indistinguishable. Overfitting mistakes noise for pattern.

04

Not all errors
are equal.

How you measure forecast quality shapes what you optimize — and what you ignore. MAE, RMSE, and MAPE tell different stories about the same mistake.

Small Large
MAE —

Mean Absolute Error

mean( |y − ŷ| )

Every error counts the same. Intuitive. Easy to explain.

RMSE —

Root Mean Squared Error

sqrt( mean( (y − ŷ)² ) )

Large errors penalized quadratically. Watch it spike as the outlier grows.

MAPE —

Mean Abs. Percentage Error

mean( |y − ŷ| / |y| ) × 100

Scale-independent. Explodes when actuals are near zero.

“There is no universal best metric. Pick the one that reflects the true cost of your errors.”

05

You cannot shuffle
time.

A random train/test split is standard for tabular ML. On a time series, it is a methodological error. Training on data from the future leaks information the model should never see.

✕ Random split (wrong)

Training data is scattered across the full timeline. The model has seen data from the future.

✓ Walk-forward (correct)

Fold 1 / 5

Training always ends before the test window. Each fold simulates a real deployment.

Training Validation Future (unseen)

“Evaluate your model the way it will be deployed: train on the past, test on the future.”

06

ARIMA needs
a stable baseline.

A stationary series has a constant mean and variance over time. ARIMA requires it. Non-stationary series must be transformed — by differencing — before modeling.

ADF: p = 0.614 — non-stationary

Clear upward trend. The mean is not constant — it grows with time. ARIMA(p,0,q) on this series would be meaningless.

“Before fitting ARIMA: test for stationarity. If ADF p > 0.05, you must difference first.”

07

ML models
don’t see time.

Tree-based models have no concept of order. You transform the series into a tabular dataset: each row is one time step, each column a lagged value or calendar feature. The model never sees the sequence — only the row.

“Feature engineering is the model for tree-based forecasting. Lag selection and leakage prevention matter more than hyperparameters.”

08

Choose your
tools wisely.

No model wins every battle. Each family makes different assumptions about your data. Knowing those assumptions is more important than memorizing hyperparameters.

Baseline

Naïve / Seasonal Naïve

Assumes: the future repeats the recent past or the same season last year.

Always compute a naïve baseline first. If your complex model cannot beat it, go back to the drawing board.

ŷt+h = yt−S+h mod S

→ Use as benchmark. Every model should beat this.

Classical

Exponential Smoothing (ETS)

Assumes: trend and seasonality evolve smoothly via exponential weighting.

A state space model. Handles multiple seasonalities. Interpretable components. Underrated in practice.

ETS(Error, Trend, Seasonality)

→ Strong seasonal patterns. Classic business forecasting.

Statistical

ARIMA / SARIMA

Assumes: the series is stationary (or can be made so by differencing d times).

Models autocorrelation structure. Requires stationarity testing (ADF, KPSS). Sensitive to outliers and structural breaks.

ARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)S

→ Complex autocorrelation. Use ACF/PACF to select p, q.

ML

LightGBM / XGBoost

Assumes: nothing explicitly — learns from engineered lag features.

Powerful with many exogenous variables. Requires careful feature engineering and strictly temporal cross-validation. Highly prone to data leakage.

f(lags, calendars, exogenous)

→ Many covariates. Non-linear patterns. Large history available.

“A simple model that understands your data beats a complex one that doesn’t.”

09

What to remember.

"Every forecast is a bet. A well-calibrated bet."

"The goal is not certainty. It is structured doubt."

"The best forecasters are not always right. They know when they are probably wrong."

I

Decompose before you predict.

Separate trend, seasonality, and noise. Each component needs a different model or assumption. Treating them as one block is the most common source of poor forecasts.

II

Widen your intervals when the ground shifts.

Structural breaks invalidate past distributions. When the context changes (regulations, crises, new products), uncertainty must be explicitly widened, not assumed away.

III

Model the uncertainty, not just the mean.

A single forecast line is a simplification. The business decision lives in the distribution: in the tails, in the worst cases, in the confidence range. Communicate intervals, not just point estimates.

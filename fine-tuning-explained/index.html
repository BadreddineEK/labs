Fine-Tuning
Explained

A pre-trained model knows the world — but it doesn't know your task. Fine-tuning bridges that gap. From full retraining to LoRA, here is every method with its trade-offs.

Published April 2026
Reading time ~12 min
Tags LoRA · PEFT · RLHF · SFT

The core idea

Why fine-tune at all?

Pre-training gives a model broad knowledge. Fine-tuning gives it a specific behaviour, style, or domain expertise — in a fraction of the compute.

01

Domain Specialisation

A base model trained on the internet does not speak medical radiology, legal contracts, or your internal data format. Fine-tuning adapts the distribution to your domain.

Domain shift

02

Instruction Following

A raw LLM completes text — it does not follow instructions. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on prompt-response pairs teaches the model to behave like an assistant.

SFT

03

Style & Format Control

Always respond in JSON? Use a specific tone? Fine-tuning hard-bakes output format into the model weights — more reliable than prompting alone.

Output control

04

Reducing Inference Cost

A fine-tuned small model (7B) can match or beat a prompted large model (70B) on a narrow task — at 10× less inference cost. This is a real production argument.

Efficiency

05

Alignment & Safety

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) is fine-tuning with a human-preference reward signal. It turned GPT-3 into ChatGPT — alignment over raw scale.

RLHF

06

Data Privacy

Calling a closed API leaks your data. A locally fine-tuned model runs entirely on-premise — critical for healthcare, finance, and enterprise use cases.

On-premise

Method selection

Train from scratch vs Fine-tune vs Prompt

Three fundamentally different strategies. The right choice depends on data availability, budget, and how different your task is from pre-training.

From scratch

Pre-training

  • Billions of tokens required
  • Millions of $ in compute
  • Full control of the distribution
  • Only justified for foundational models
  • OpenAI, Google, Mistral territory
Recommended

Fine-tuning

  • 1k – 100k labelled examples
  • Hours to days of training
  • Modifies (some) model weights
  • Best accuracy on narrow tasks
  • SFT, LoRA, QLoRA, RLHF
No training

Prompting / RAG

  • Zero labelled examples needed
  • Instant to deploy
  • Weights unchanged
  • Limited by context window
  • Best for dynamic knowledge

The key innovation

LoRA — Low-Rank Adaptation

Instead of updating all 7 billion parameters, LoRA freezes the original weights and injects tiny trainable matrices. 99% of memory saved, almost no quality loss.

Why low-rank works

Weight updates during fine-tuning have a low intrinsic rank — the update ΔW lies in a small subspace of the full parameter space. LoRA exploits this directly.

QLoRA = LoRA + Quantization

QLoRA loads the base model in 4-bit NF4, keeping adapters in 16-bit. A 65B model fits on a single 48GB GPU. Fine-tuning on a consumer setup becomes possible.

Merging at inference

Once trained, LoRA adapters can be merged into the original weights (W = W₀ + BA), adding zero inference latency. Or keep them separate and swap tasks at runtime.

Step by step

How a fine-tuning run works

From raw data to a deployed model — the 6 stages every practitioner goes through.

1

Collect & format your dataset

For instruction tuning, you need prompt-completion pairs. The standard is JSONL with {"instruction", "input", "output"} fields. Quality beats quantity — 1k clean examples beat 100k noisy ones.

{"instruction": "Summarise this report", "input": "Q4 sales declined 12%...", "output": "Q4 sales fell 12% due to..."}
2

Choose your base model

The base model determines the ceiling of your fine-tune. Llama 3, Mistral 7B, Qwen 2.5 are the go-to open-source bases. For classification or embedding tasks, BERT-class models are often better.

from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.3")
3

Configure the PEFT adapter (LoRA)

Define rank r, alpha (scaling), and which modules to target. Typically: q_proj, v_proj in attention layers. Dropout 0.05–0.1 to regularise.

from peft import LoraConfig config = LoraConfig(r=16, lora_alpha=32, target_modules=["q_proj","v_proj"], lora_dropout=0.05, bias="none", task_type="CAUSAL_LM")
4

Train with SFTTrainer

Hugging Face TRL's SFTTrainer handles data collation, gradient accumulation, and logging. Use bf16 on modern GPUs. A typical run: 3 epochs, learning rate 2e-4, cosine scheduler.

from trl import SFTTrainer trainer = SFTTrainer(model=model, train_dataset=dataset, peft_config=config, ...) trainer.train()
5

Evaluate on held-out set

Track loss on a validation split. For generation tasks, use ROUGE, BLEU or human evaluation. For classification, standard accuracy/F1. Watch for overfitting on small datasets.

trainer.evaluate() # => {"eval_loss": 0.83, "eval_rouge1": 0.61, ...}
6

Merge & deploy

Merge the LoRA adapters into the base model for zero-latency inference, then export to GGUF (llama.cpp) or ONNX. Deploy via vLLM, Ollama, or Hugging Face Inference Endpoints.

merged = model.merge_and_unload() merged.save_pretrained("./my-fine-tuned-model")

The math

Key equations

Four equations that define the modern fine-tuning landscape.

LoRA weight update

h = W₀x + ΔWx = W₀x + BAx
where B ∈ ℝ^(d×r), A ∈ ℝ^(r×d), r ≪ d

A is initialised with Gaussian, B with zeros — so ΔW = 0 at start. Only A and B are updated during training.

LoRA scaling

ΔW = (α / r) · BA

α controls the magnitude of the adaptation. Setting α = 2r is a common heuristic. Increasing α boosts the adapter signal relative to the frozen weights.

RLHF reward objective

max E[r(x,y)] - β · KL[π_θ(y|x) || π_ref(y|x)]

Maximise reward from the human preference model while staying close (KL divergence) to the original SFT policy. β controls the balance.

DPO loss (simpler RLHF)

L_DPO = -log σ(β log[π_θ(y_w)/π_ref(y_w)] - β log[π_θ(y_l)/π_ref(y_l)])

Direct Preference Optimisation skips the reward model entirely. Train directly on (chosen, rejected) pairs — used in Llama 3, Mistral, and most 2026 alignment pipelines.

History

From transfer learning to PEFT

How fine-tuning evolved from a niche technique to the core of modern AI deployment.

2018

BERT & GPT — transfer learning takes off

Pre-train on large corpora, fine-tune on downstream tasks. Full fine-tuning on all layers becomes the standard recipe.

2021

Prefix Tuning & Adapter layers

First PEFT methods: freeze the model, add small trainable modules. Showed that <1% of parameters can carry most of the fine-tuning signal.

2021

InstructGPT — RLHF alignment

OpenAI fine-tunes GPT-3 with human feedback. SFT + reward model + PPO. A 1.3B InstructGPT outperforms a 175B GPT-3 on human preference. Alignment > scale.

2022

LoRA (Hu et al., Microsoft)

Low-Rank Adaptation shows that fine-tuning with 0.1–1% of parameters matches full fine-tuning on most tasks. The PEFT landscape shifts entirely.

2023

QLoRA — democratisation

4-bit quantisation + LoRA. A 65B model on one consumer GPU. Fine-tuning stops being a hyperscaler exclusive. The open-source wave begins.

2024–26

DPO, ORPO, SimPO — RLHF without RL

Alignment without reward models. DPO, used in Llama 3 and Mistral, achieves RLHF quality at SFT cost. The standard recipe in 2026 production pipelines.
